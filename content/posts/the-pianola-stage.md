## A Brief History of the Piano

Before pianos, we had [the clavichord](https://youtu.be/LbrKmwZGynE?si=2n7wELAc6455j3EY&t=71)
and [the harpsichord](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2unB-S9JXV8).

The clavichord was primarily a practice/personal instrument since it wasn't 
loud enough for performances. The harpsichord was able to achieve greater volume
but, due to fundamental differences in how the instrument interacted with the
strings, was unable to produce variations in volume.

Fast forward a couple hundred years to the introduction of the _gravicèmbalo con piano e forte_.

I love this. Did you get the translation? 

> Harpsichord with soft and loud

Laziness (I assume), being a fundamental human trait, led to the eventual shortening of _gravicèmbalo con piano e forte_ to _pianoforte_, and eventually _**piano**_. 

## Why Does this Matter?

To answer that question, we need to go just a bit further.

To motivate my point, let's focus on the evolution of the player piano. 

Once we reached **piano** levels of enlightenment, people eventually began to experiment
with devices that were able to play back music on the piano without the need for a human
to press the keys.

[Modern player pianos](https://youtu.be/yLxdaM7jQ7w?si=rPAg5F4rITnjPJql&t=171) are
nearly indistinguishable from their non-player counterparts. The electronic
and mechanical components are tightly integrated along-side the "normal" piano hardware.

This approach has a delightful side effect that the piano can still be played by a human.

But it wasn't always so. 

![The Pianola - a device that plays the piano](/blog/images/pianola.jpg)

We started with a fundamentally different approach. 

Instead of trying to make a piano that played itself, we made
a device that played the piano ([the _Pianola_](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D5aNuSmL5Ek)).

**We looked at how pianos were played [by humans] and applied the same pattern
to the pursuit of an automatic\* player mechanism.**

_*Aside: The Pianola couldn't play the piano by itself. It required a [human to play the Pianola](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fbIuYArTfrk) which, in turn, played the piano._

## The Point

At long last, I can make my point: 

**Software development lifecycle evolution is at the Pianola stage.***

_*maybe_

But is this a bad thing? 

While the Pianola model was highly unusable for the player piano application,
it had advantages. It could play _any piano_ (with a standard key layout.)

Maybe software development should evolve in the Pianola direction.

Maybe we need a fundamentally different approach.

Maybe we need to rethink the entire process, which was largely built with an assumption that humans are typing code.

What is the _modern player piano_ equivalent of an autonomous software development system?
